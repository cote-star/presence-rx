The One-Liner

Presence Rx: find your brand's blind spots in AI answers. We turn Peec visibility data into an auditable Presence Verdict for marketers — with receipts at every step.

The Problem

When someone asks ChatGPT or Gemini "best minimalist tech brands," the AI gives an answer. That answer mentions some brands, ignores others, and frames each one with specific traits. Nothing Phone — a brand built on minimalist transparent design — appears in only 6% of those answers. Apple appears in 39%.

A marketer can check Peec and see "we're mentioned in 20% of prompts overall." But that number alone doesn't tell them:

  • Where do they dominate? 72% visibility in Smartphone Design — but only 6% in Minimalist Hardware.
  • What trait is the brand missing? AI answers associate "minimalist" with Apple, not Nothing Phone.
  • What's safe to claim? Saying "Nothing Phone is the minimalist tech leader" would be wrong — the data contradicts it.
  • What to actually do about it? Create minimalist positioning content? Publish Nothing Ear reviews? Just monitor?

Most tools stop at the visibility number. Presence Rx goes further.

What We Build: The Presence Verdict

A verdict pack — a set of structured artifacts that a marketer can read, trust, and act on:

  • PRESENCE_VERDICT.md — the full diagnosis: where the brand appears, who owns which prompts, what associations are missing, what's strong vs weak.
  • ACTION_BRIEF.md — the short executive output: what to do this week, what messaging to change, what content to create, what not to claim, and what prompts to monitor.
  • EVIDENCE_LEDGER.json — every claim mapped to its sources (Peec data, public web evidence, Gemini analysis) with confidence grades.
  • manifest.json — metadata: which Peec snapshot, which taxonomy version, when generated, how fresh.

How the Pipeline Works

1
Ingest from Peec MCP Pull the visibility data for Nothing Phone and its 9 competitors (Apple, Samsung, Google, Sony, Bose, Pixel, Xiaomi, OnePlus, Logitech). Peec gives us 50 prompts across 5 topics. Brand reports add visibility (20% overall), SoV (8%), sentiment (64), and position (2.4 — best in the field).
Peec is the source of truth for visibility. Nothing else overrides it.
2
Map Peec Topics to Parent Themes Peec already groups prompts into 5 topics: Smartphone Design, Mobile Ecosystem, Consumer Tech Innovation, Minimalist Hardware, Wireless Audio. We layer our campaign taxonomy on top. Key finding: Nothing Phone owns 1 topic (72% vis) but has critical blind spots in 4 others (1–12% vis). Each topic gets an evidence tier and decision bucket.
3
Enrich with Tavily Tavily searches the public web to find supporting or contradicting evidence. Does Apple dominate "minimalist desk setup" search results? Does Nothing Phone have any dedicated minimalist positioning content? This finds proof gaps — things the brand should have publicly but doesn't, especially on editorial gap domains (rtings, whathifi, dezeen, yankodesign).
Tavily owns public evidence. It doesn't override Peec visibility.
4
Analyze with Gemini Gemini classifies prompts into parent marketing themes (like "buyer friction" or "implementation support"), identifies perception gaps (what trait the brand is missing), and drafts scenario recommendations. We use a parent-topic taxonomy — candidate topics get classified into stable marketing themes, confidence-filtered, and only accepted labels flow into the action brief.
Gemini owns analysis language. It doesn't own visibility truth.
5
Apply Guardrails This is the key differentiator. Every finding goes through a three-field check (see below), then gets assigned a decision bucket: act now, test next, monitor, deprioritize, or block. At least one finding must be visibly blocked or downgraded in the demo.
6
Generate Artifacts The pipeline produces the study, action brief, evidence ledger, and manifest. Markdown artifacts are generated from structured JSON (the study SSOT), not hand-written. A dashboard reads the same JSON.

The Guardrail Engine (Our Differentiator)

Every finding passes through three separate checks:

CheckQuestionValues
Input GateCan this finding even enter analysis?passed / failed / skipped
Evidence TierHow strong is the evidence?strong / moderate / limited / blocked
Publication StatusCan it appear in executive output?publishable / directional_with_caveat / diagnostics_only / blocked

The method ladder determines evidence tier:

EvidenceRuleAllowed Language
strong5+ Peec observations, 2+ methods agree, no conflict"Prioritize this action"
moderate2–4 observations, some agreement"Test this action"
limitedThin Peec data, only public evidence"Explore this hypothesis"
blockedContradictory evidence or overstatement risk"Do not claim this yet"

Any system that only shows positive recommendations looks like it's making stuff up. Showing what was blocked is what makes it credible.

Why This Approach?

Every design decision answers one question: how do we win a hackathon track where most teams will build a generic AI marketing dashboard?

The Competitive Landscape

Peec gives every team the same starting point: AI-answer visibility data. Most teams will do one of these:

  • Dashboard team: Pipe Peec numbers into charts. "You are mentioned 33% of the time." Looks polished, says nothing actionable.
  • Content generator team: Feed Peec data to an LLM, output blog post ideas. Generic, no evidence, no guardrails.
  • Chatbot team: "Ask questions about your AI visibility." Interactive but shallow — no structured output a marketer can act on.

We are none of these. We produce a structured Presence Verdict with auditable evidence, guardrails, and an explicit proof chain. The judges see discipline, not just a demo.

Design Decision: Evidence-Backed Verdict Pack

Alternative: Generate a Report

Just have Gemini write a nice marketing report from Peec data. Fast to build, looks professional.

Why we rejected it: No traceability. The report looks authoritative but no one can verify any claim. A judge asks "where does this come from?" and there's no answer. This is what every AI-generated report already does.

Our Choice: Presence Verdict + Evidence Ledger

Every claim has a claim_id, evidence refs, source of record, and a guardrail decision. The verdict and action brief are generated from a single source-of-truth JSON.

Why this wins: Traceability is the feature. "Click any recommendation, see the receipt." No other hackathon team will do this because it's harder to build.

Design Decision: Guardrails as the Differentiator

Alternative: Show Only Positive Recommendations

Typical AI output: "Here are 5 things you should do." No qualification, no blocked claims, no honesty about data limits.

Why we rejected it: It looks like every other AI tool. Judges have seen this pattern 100 times. It also overclaims — if the data is thin, the recommendation is noise.

Our Choice: Block Weak Claims Visibly

The system refuses to promote a finding when methods conflict or coverage is too thin. Blocked claims are shown with reasons and safe rewrites.

Why this wins: It's counterintuitive — showing what the system won't say is more impressive than showing what it will. Judges remember the "no" because every other team only says "yes."

Design Decision: Peec as Anchor, Not Just Data Source

Alternative: Use Peec as One Input Among Many

Treat Peec, Gemini, and Tavily as equal sources. Blend everything into a unified score.

Why we rejected it: It dilutes Peec. The track judges want to see Peec used well, not buried in a blend. It also creates a mess — if everything is a source of truth, nothing is.

Our Choice: Strict Source-of-Record Hierarchy

Peec owns visibility. Gemini owns analysis language. Tavily owns public evidence. No tool overrides another's domain. Every field has a declared source.

Why this wins: The Peec PM sees her product positioned as the credible anchor. Judges see clean architecture. The source-of-record map is a visible artifact, not a hidden assumption.

Design Decision: Prompt Clusters, Not Raw Prompts

Alternative: Show Individual Prompt Results

List every Peec observation one by one. 42 rows of "this prompt mentioned you in position 3."

Why we rejected it: Not actionable. A marketer can't act on 42 individual prompts. And it looks like a data dump, not a product.

Our Choice: Cluster into Actionable Units

Group similar prompts into clusters. Each cluster has an owner, evidence tier, decision bucket, and campaign type. The pipeline funnel shows 42 → 9 → 3.

Why this wins: The funnel is the demo moment. "We started with 42 prompts and the system narrowed it to 3 actionable moves." That's a story. A list of 42 rows is not.

Design Decision: Campaign Taxonomy

Alternative: Just Show Clusters

Prompt clusters are enough. Skip the parent-topic and campaign-type layers.

Why we rejected it: Marketers think in campaigns, not prompt clusters. "brand_marketing" means something; "cluster:minimalist-hardware" doesn't. Without the taxonomy, the output feels like an engineering artifact, not a marketing tool.

Our Choice: Three-Level Taxonomy

Prompt cluster → parent topic → campaign type → campaign category. When type-level evidence is thin, fall back to category and say so.

Why this wins: Adds intellectual depth without complexity. Shows the system is precise when evidence supports it and conservative when it doesn't. That graceful degradation is what separates a prototype from a product.

We're betting that in a room full of AI dashboards and content generators, the team that shows discipline — evidence chains, blocked claims, source-of-record separation, and graceful degradation — will stand out.

This bet is strong because:

  • Hackathon judges are tired of "here's what AI generated." They reward why and how.
